Data Collection¶

Before we can even begin thinking about data, we need to first choose a topic of interest and find the data. Depending on what you are in search of, there are endless online resources with terabytes of data tables ready for use. The team at AwesomeData has done an amazing job compiling a massive list of external links to datasets on other webpages here: https://github.com/awesomedata/awesome-public-datasets. These are all free and publicly available, with topics ranging from agriculture to video games. The provided github page acts as a hub for sourcing large data sets, and even links to other compilations such as Kraggle. Some other sources of locating interesting datasets are from government websites like: https://www.data.gov/, and https://data.virginia.gov/. The national government, states, and even local governments make data universally accessible for browsing.

Scraping the Data¶

If downloading readily available data files and importing them seems too easy of a challenge for you, it's time to get into the background behind websites. Assuming you have a Jupyter notebook ready with a python file, all you need to do is import a few libraries in preparation for scraping the data out of websites:

from bs4 import BeautifulSoup
import requests
import pandas as pd

The requests library is necessary to pull the html code out from a webpage by sending an HTTP request:

web = requests.get('https://website.com/sample_data.html')

Because the response for this request will be extremely jumbled and difficult to parse by normal means, the BeautifulSoup library allows us to clean up the response and make it presentable:

soup = BeautifulSoup(web.content)
print(soup.prettify())

Then, you can find the data table you identified in the previous step easily:

data_table = soup.find('table', class_='table table-striped table-responsive-md')

The last piece of this puzzle is to utilize the Pandas library to structure our data into a presentable table in code. Pandas uses a structure called the DataFrame, which has numerous constructors and parameters to fine tune the extracted data into a DataFrame. When compared to NumPy, which is more oriented towards mathematically manipulating data through operations, Pandas is much more useful for restructuring, cleaning, and organizing data. The best way to learn more about the essential pandas library is to visit the official documentation here: https://pandas.pydata.org/docs/reference/api/pandas.DataFrame.html.

Cleaning the Data¶

In the traditional sense, cleaning the data would require a bit more effort than it might appear in this tutorial. The Kaggle dataset that has been chosen does not have any missing entries, or entirely redundant information that could be compressed. Generally, you would parse the rows through some form of loop, where each missing entry would be replaced with a NaN or something similar to indicate the absence of data. This allows us to standardize all missing entries so they can either be ignored or handled in processes later as a single entity. An example of cleansing could follow as shown:

for index, row in data_table.iterrows():
    # Replace missing entries with NaN
    row['col1'] = row['col1'].replace('------', 'NaN')
    row['col2'] = row['col2'].replace('****', 'NaN')
    row['col3'] = row['col3'].replace('--.--', 'NaN')

In the specific case of our dataset, I noticed that the two columns indicating whether a streamer was partnered, or if their channel was marked as mature, were insignificant in their impact on the research. For example, we can see here that only 230 channels were marked as mature from the entire dataset of 1,000 entries. Also, only 22 channels were not partnered in the entire dataset. While an analysis could be made for how a mature filter affects viewership and followers gained, it is not in the interest of this tutorial to examine the impact of a feature belonging to 23% of the data.

Managing the Data¶

Dropping and Sorting¶

Dropping columns altogether is a simple matter with pandas DataFrames, as we can make use of the drop method, and include any columns we want to remove in a list, with the axis being 1. Now that the columns are dropped, we are free to sort our data any way we choose to. Sorting is just as easy as dropping, with the _sortvalues method. The syntax requires us to list the columns to sort by in order of priority, and then choose whether or not this will be an asceding sort. Due to the fact that I would like to focus primarily on the impact of streaming time, increased followage, and viewer retention, I can set my priorities accordingly. The only issue with sorting is that the indices of the DataFrame object will be jumbled, which we can reorganize with the _resetindex method.

Filtering the Data by Language¶

Now that we have identified the main goals of examining the dataset, cleaned it up, and organized it, we can start isolating some subsets. Although 1,000 rows is nowhere near a massive amount of data, we can further refine our DataFrame by creating subsets of rows that are similar to each other. The most glaring subset is that of language, so that is what I will be separating by below. Six of the most popular languages were selected based on the code below. A simple list was made from the set of languages in the DataFrame's Language column. This list was used to populate a dictionary with all the languages, and then a loop incremented each language by one when encountered in the column. The last step was to sort the languages and note down the top six, because they are relatively close in number. English channels will be the focus of this tutorial.

Representation & Analysis¶

Plotting the Data¶

After we have succesfully determined what data we are interested in from the entire dataset, it is time to visually depict it. There are numerous options for showing off the data in question such as line graphs, bar graphs, violin plots, scatter plots, and many more. I have chosen to show a scatterplot to demonstrate some trends in the data, and clusters that may appear where the values are primarily concentrated. Also, while outliers are usually ignored or unhelpful, I am also aiming to examine these special cases and explain their peculiar situations. Before we plot, however, we need to know why we are plotting specific variables. Is there something in particular we are looking to prove, or have a predefined belief on? If that is the case, it's time to form a hypothesis! An early hypothesis I have from seeing this data is that simply streaming more does not equate to an increase in followers. The null-hypothesis is the opposite of this assumption, and what we are looking to disprove.

With a hypothesis in mind, all that's left to do is plot our two variables and analyze them. Plotting is done using the matplotlib library, which makes it easy for us to simply plug in our variables and view them without any extra effort. The only setup we need to do is changing the x and y labels, as well as adding a title. If you are looking for some more insight on setting up scatterplots, here is a tutorial from the official documentation: https://matplotlib.org/stable/gallery/shapes_and_collections/scatter.html.

Intermediate Analysis¶

What we see here is interesting: a majority of channels that brought in the most followers in the one year period streamed for much less time than those who did not gain many more followers. To confirm this, we can visualize the linear regression of the plot and analyze the plot. If there is a downwards trend, we have successfully proved our simple hypothesis, and rejected the null hypothesis. Thanks to the seaborn library we imported earlier, regression lines can be done with around three lines of code, and customized greatly. More information on the seaborn library and its plotting capabilities can be found at: https://seaborn.pydata.org/tutorial/regression.html.

Confirmation of Results¶

As expected, we can see a negative trend in the scatter plot, even though the peculiar dataset does not allow for a perfect regression. However, we can make some assumptions from this plot and theorize further on other variables. First, it looks like at least one outlier came out with approximately 600K new followers throughouth the year, and was rewarded for their streaming time. If we actually pinpoint this channel, it is ESL_CSGO, an eSports broadcast for one of the most popular franchises in gaming history that hosts tournaments with massive prize pools. Their immense followers gained statistic can be explained by the resurgence in the CSGO scene, and stream time is easily accounted for with year-round competitions and reruns. The takeaway here is that not all of our conclusions can be drawn from the dataset itself, as there might be extra research or background knowledge required to fill in the gaps for outliers. More importantly, we have debunked the popular myth that simply streaming more will result in a larger audience.

The Four Plots & Final Analysis¶

At a basic level, we can make observations from the regressions for each of the plots in order. As average viewers go up, so do new followers. As watch time goes up, once again new followers go up as well. In the reverse, as followers are gained, average viewership also increases. Lastly, as followers are gained, watch time follows suit. Many of these seem to be very obvious conclusions: more people watching results in interested viewers following the channels, and if more individuals follow the channels they are likely to keep watching and increase viewership. Although they are very apparent, I chose these plots to show the cyclical relationship in the Twitch ecosystem, and what factors break into this cycle every so often that we cannot see here.

Streamers can have spontaneous breakout streams where they pull record numbers and ride the hype for a while. These streams can be from new game releases, global trends, current events coverage, or even social media posts. You might be inclined to believe that the Peak Viewers column could have helped us in our original goal to see this, but we are missing crucial time data. Without knowing when specific 'peak streams' occurred, we cannot analyze their lasting effects on average viewership gained. A future recommendation would be to focus on a single channel from this dataset, isolate their highest peak streams to see resulting viewer retention (average viewer increase), and provide streamers with insight on their channels. Although this would be the preferred method to measure viewer retention, we can still confidently say from our plots that streamers who bring in new followers through these 'boom' periods, are generally able to retain their viewership and increase their average viewer count, as well as watch time.
